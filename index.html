Our Mission

We conserve the lands and waters on which all life depends

Founded in 1951, The Nature Conservancy is a global conservation organization dedicated to conserving the lands and waters on which all life depends. Guided by science, we create innovative, on-the-ground solutions to our world's toughest challenges so that nature and people can thrive together.

We're tackling climate change, conserving lands, waters and oceans at unprecedented scale, providing food and water sustainably and helping make cities more sustainable. Working in 72 countries, we use a collaborative approach that engages local communities, governments, the private sector, and other partners.

Our Story

For more than 70 years, we've been working to protect the lands and waters on which all life depends

Founded in 1951, The Nature Conservancy began as a small group of scientists and conservationists who came together to protect a special place in New York. Since then, we've grown to become one of the most effective and wide-reaching environmental organizations in the world, thanks to the support of more than one million members and the dedicated efforts of our diverse staff.

Our approach is rooted in science and guided by the belief that conservation is about people. We know that to solve the complex environmental challenges facing our planet, we must work collaboratively—with communities, governments, businesses and other organizations—to find solutions that work for both nature and people.

Our History

Key milestones in our conservation journey

1951

The Nature Conservancy Founded

A small group of scientists and conservationists come together to form The Nature Conservancy, with the mission to protect ecologically important lands and waters.

1961

First International Project

We expand beyond U.S. borders with our first international project in Canada, protecting the Cowichan Garry Oak Preserve in British Columbia.

1988

Debt-for-Nature Swap

We pioneer the debt-for-nature swap concept, converting foreign debt into funds for conservation in Bolivia, establishing a model used worldwide.

2007

Plant a Billion Trees

We launch our ambitious reforestation initiative in Brazil's Atlantic Forest, aiming to plant one billion trees by 2025.

2020

Global Conservation Goals

We announce ambitious 2030 goals to address climate change and biodiversity loss, including protecting 30% of the planet by 2030.

Addressing the Climate Crisis

Our Climate Change Program is at the forefront of developing and implementing nature-based solutions to the climate crisis. We recognize that nature can provide at least 30% of the mitigation needed to limit global temperature rise to 1.5°C by 2030.

Our Approach

We focus on three key strategies to address climate change:

  • Natural Climate Solutions: Protecting, restoring and improving the management of forests, wetlands and grasslands to store carbon and reduce emissions.
  • Climate-Smart Agriculture: Working with farmers and ranchers to implement practices that increase carbon storage in soils while improving productivity.
  • Renewable Energy Development: Advancing clean energy in ways that benefit both nature and people through smart siting and biodiversity protections.

Key Initiatives

Impact to Date

Since 2005, our climate initiatives have:

  • Protected over 50 million acres of forests that store carbon
  • Supported the adoption of climate-smart agricultural practices on 12 million acres
  • Helped avoid 1.5 billion metric tons of CO2 emissions
  • Trained over 5,000 conservation professionals in climate adaptation strategies

Get Involved

You can support our climate work through donations, advocacy, or by making climate-friendly choices in your daily life. Every action counts in the fight against climate change.

Hope for Coral Reefs

Great Barrier Reef, Australia
Published: June 15, 2023
Ocean Conservation

In a groundbreaking study funded by The Nature Conservancy, marine biologists have identified several coral species that show remarkable resilience to rising ocean temperatures. These "super corals" could hold the key to restoring damaged reef ecosystems around the world.

The Discovery

During a five-year research project on Australia's Great Barrier Reef, scientists noticed that certain coral colonies were surviving in reef areas where most other corals had bleached and died during marine heatwaves. Genetic analysis revealed these resilient corals have unique adaptations that help them withstand higher temperatures.

Why It Matters

Coral reefs are among the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth, supporting about 25% of all marine species. They also protect coastlines from storms and provide food and income for millions of people. But climate change has caused mass coral bleaching events that have damaged reefs worldwide.

"These resilient corals give us hope that some reefs may be able to adapt to warmer oceans," said Dr. Emma Rodriguez, lead scientist on the project. "By identifying and protecting these 'super corals,' we can help reefs survive the climate challenges ahead."

Next Steps

The research team is now working on several fronts:

  • Expanding surveys to identify more resilient coral colonies
  • Studying the genetic and microbial factors that contribute to thermal tolerance
  • Developing coral nurseries to propagate resilient strains
  • Transplanting resilient corals to damaged reef areas

At the same time, the team emphasizes that reducing greenhouse gas emissions remains critical to giving corals the best chance of survival.

How You Can Help

You can support coral reef conservation by:

  • Reducing your carbon footprint
  • Choosing sustainable seafood
  • Using reef-safe sunscreens
  • Supporting marine conservation organizations

"This discovery gives us new tools to protect coral reefs, but we need everyone's help to address the root causes of climate change," said Dr. Rodriguez.

Indigenous Communities Lead Amazon Protection

Peruvian Amazon
Published: May 2, 2023
Indigenous Conservation

In the heart of the Peruvian Amazon, the Shipibo-Conibo people are demonstrating how indigenous knowledge and modern conservation science can work together to protect the rainforest. With support from The Nature Conservancy, they've established a community-managed conservation area that safeguards both biodiversity and their traditional way of life.

A Model of Community Conservation

The 200,000-acre Maroti Conservation Concession was established in 2018 after five years of planning and negotiation with the Peruvian government. Unlike traditional protected areas managed by government agencies, this concession is entirely managed by the Shipibo-Conibo community.

"This is our ancestral land," explains community leader Mateo Inuma. "We've always protected it, but now we have legal recognition and tools to do it even better."

The community employs a mix of traditional practices and modern technology:

  • Using drones and GPS to monitor illegal logging
  • Documenting medicinal plants with scientists
  • Combining indigenous fire management with satellite data
  • Developing sustainable forest products for income

Proven Results

In its first five years, the Maroti Concession has:

  • Reduced deforestation by 85% compared to surrounding areas
  • Documented over 2,000 plant and animal species
  • Created 50 jobs in sustainable forest management
  • Trained 30 community forest rangers

Perhaps most importantly, it's become a model for other indigenous communities seeking to protect their lands.

Challenges Remain

Despite their success, the Shipibo-Conibo face ongoing challenges:

  • Pressure from illegal loggers and miners
  • Climate change altering traditional weather patterns
  • Balancing conservation with community needs

"We're showing that conservation can go hand-in-hand with sustainable development," says Inuma. "But we need continued support to keep our forest standing."

A Global Movement

Similar indigenous-led conservation initiatives are emerging worldwide, supported by research showing that indigenous peoples manage or have tenure rights over lands that contain about 80% of the world's biodiversity.

"Supporting indigenous land rights isn't just about justice—it's one of the most effective conservation strategies we have," says Carlos Rodriguez, TNC's Indigenous Partnerships Director.

Restoring Forests in Brazil

Atlantic Forest, Brazil
Since 2008
73M trees planted

The Atlantic Forest Initiative

Brazil's Atlantic Forest is one of the most biodiverse—and most threatened—ecosystems on Earth. Originally covering 330 million acres, only about 12% remains today. Since 2008, our Plant a Billion Trees initiative has been working to restore this critical forest.

Why It Matters

The Atlantic Forest is home to thousands of species found nowhere else, including 60% of Brazil's threatened animals. It also provides clean water for 70% of Brazil's population and stores massive amounts of carbon.

Our Approach

We don't just plant trees—we restore entire ecosystems:

  • Science-Based Restoration: Using native species that recreate natural forest ecosystems
  • Community Partnerships: Working with local farmers and landowners
  • Long-Term Commitment: Monitoring and maintaining plantings for years
  • Policy Support: Advocating for forest protection laws

Impact of Your Support

Thanks to donor support, we've achieved:

  • Planted over 73 million native trees
  • Restored 75,000 acres of forest
  • Created 1,500 green jobs
  • Protected watersheds for 20 million people
  • Sequestered 10 million tons of CO2

Looking Ahead

Our goal is to plant 1 billion trees by 2025. With your help, we can:

  • Restore 1 million acres of forest
  • Connect fragmented habitats for wildlife
  • Secure clean water for cities like São Paulo
  • Create thousands more green jobs

How You Can Help

Every dollar plants a tree and helps restore this vital ecosystem:

  • $1 plants one tree
  • $50 restores a small plot
  • $250 restores an acre
  • $1,000 restores critical wildlife corridors
